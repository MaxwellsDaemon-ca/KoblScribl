“Then it’s not about being right,” Wolf said. “It’s about being *convincing*.” His smile returned, easy, practiced. “People don’t follow the best idea. They follow the one they believe in.”

“And how do you make them believe?”

“Tell them what they already want to hear,” he said, without hesitation. Then, just as quickly, his expression shifted, a flicker of something more genuine cutting through. “Or –” He caught himself, the word hanging there for a fraction too long. “– you try something better.”

“Better?”

“Honest,” he said, and this time it stuck. Not as smooth. Not as easy. Just… there.

The man’s gaze lingered on that for a moment, like he was weighing it.

“And does that work?” he asked.

Wolf huffed softly, a quiet laugh that didn’t quite reach his eyes. “I’m working on it.”

“Of course you are.”

That should have sounded encouraging. It didn’t.

Wolf’s eyes flicked once more around the room, slower this time, more deliberate, like he was looking for something he hadn’t found yet. “You keep saying things like that,” he said. “Like you already know how this plays out.”

“I know how things tend to play out.”

“Yeah?” He tilted his head slightly. “And how’s that?”

The smile he got in return was small, controlled.

“Context.”

Wolf held his gaze for a second longer, then let out a quiet breath through his nose, something between amusement and acknowledgment. “Right,” he said. “Figured.”

He shifted his weight, not backing off, not pressing forward, just settling into place like he’d decided how much of himself he was going to show. “So what, you get what you need, and I just… what, wake up somewhere else?”

“In a manner of speaking.”

“That’s not reassuring.”

“It’s not meant to be.”

That, at least, was honest.

Wolf gave a small nod, accepting that more easily than he should have. “Alright,” he said. “Then I guess we’re done here.”

“We are.”

No buildup. No warning.

The room didn’t change, not in any way he could track. No shift in the walls, no flicker in the light, no distortion to grab onto and understand. It simply… stopped being relevant. One moment he was there, standing across from someone who clearly knew more than he was saying, already working through angles he didn’t have enough information to complete, and the next –

There was nothing left to read.

---

The world didn’t shift.

It resolved.

One moment there was nothing – no room, no boundary, no sense of placement – and the next there was a stage beneath them, broad and polished, its surface catching the light in long, clean reflections that stretched farther than they should have. Twelve figures stood upon it, spaced with deliberate care, close enough to be aware of one another, far enough that no one was forced into immediate contact. None of them had arrived. Not really. It felt more like the space had finished assembling around them.

The understanding came with it.

Not spoken, not taught – remembered. A game show. Reputation. Ranking. Observation. The rules settled cleanly, fully formed, slipping into place with a certainty that didn’t ask permission. No harm permitted – not claw, not magic, not strength. Power restricted, not gone, but held just out of reach, like a motion stopped halfway through. It didn’t feel imposed. It felt… already decided.

Sisu reacted first in motion if not in comprehension, long, serpentine body shifting in a smooth curve as she turned, scales catching the light in soft blues and violets. Her ears perked, eyes bright, taking everything in at once without narrowing it down. “Oh!” she said, the sound quick and genuine, her expression already warming. “Okay, wow – hi!” One paw lifted in an easy wave, like this was just another meeting that had gotten slightly out of hand.

A soft tap of boots answered somewhere off to the side as Puss in Boots settled into place, small but impossibly self-assured, hat tilted at a practiced angle. One paw drifted toward the hilt of his blade before stopping, claws flexing once as the restriction caught up with him. His smile held anyway, bright and theatrical, though it sharpened just slightly as he adjusted.

Nick Wilde didn’t rush. Lean, rust-red fur catching the stage light, he lifted one paw to smooth an invisible crease from his sleeve, green eyes already moving across the others with quiet precision. His posture stayed loose, casual to the point of disinterest, but there was nothing unfocused about him, the slight tilt of his head marking distances, reactions, who was watching whom. The smirk followed a second later, settling in like he’d decided this was something he could work with.

Near the edge of the group, Rimuru Tempest sat… still. A small, translucent sphere of pale blue slime, barely rising above the stage, its surface catching the light with a soft, watery sheen. No limbs, no posture to read, no obvious expression – and yet the faintest ripple passed across its surface as it shifted, subtle, controlled, like something inside it had taken note of everything at once and decided not to react just yet.

Tigress moved next, and unlike Sisu, there was nothing open about it. Her striped frame settled low and grounded, paws placing with deliberate precision as she adjusted her stance, shoulders squared, gaze cutting sharply across the stage. She didn’t test the space – she tested the people, her eyes lingering just long enough to register size, balance, readiness. The restriction sat with her visibly, in the tightening of her jaw, the subtle recalibration of a body that expected to act and now… couldn’t.

Legoshi stood a little apart without meaning to, tall frame slightly hunched, grey fur catching the light in uneven patches. His hands hovered uncertainly at his sides, claws flexing once before stilling as his gaze flicked up to meet someone else’s and dropped just as quickly. The understanding didn’t settle cleanly for him – it lingered, catching, leaving him caught between reacting and holding himself still.

Twilight Sparkle remained motionless longer than most, wings drawn in close, violet eyes unfocused for just a fraction too long as the information arranged itself in her mind. When she blinked, her attention snapped outward all at once, not to the individuals first but to the structure of the space itself, the placement, the symmetry, the intention behind it. Her expression tightened slightly as she realized she hadn’t been the one to derive any of it.

A flick of movement, controlled and minimal, marked Renamon, who stood with arms loosely folded, tall and still as a held breath. Her golden eyes tracked across the stage with quiet intensity, not lingering long enough to be obvious, but not missing anything either. The only sign of motion was the faint shift of her tail behind her, slow, deliberate, alive.

Stolas drew inward slightly, tall avian frame folding just enough to seem smaller than he was, dark plumage settling unevenly along his shoulders. His gaze lingered on each figure longer than the others’, searching rather than assessing, as though trying to place familiarity where there was none. One hand lifted briefly, uncertain, before dropping again, his posture attempting composure and not quite reaching it.

A low, contained presence pulled attention next as Kurama shifted, his massive form compressed into something the space would allow. No longer mountainous, but still large – roughly the size of a horse – his nine tails settled slowly behind him, each movement controlled, deliberate. The restriction did nothing to lessen the *weight* of him, the sense of something vast held tightly in check.

Mr. Wolf stood easy among them, tall, grey-furred, shoulders relaxed, paws loose at his sides. He didn’t announce himself, didn’t move more than necessary, just let his gaze drift across the group at an unhurried pace. His expression stayed open, almost friendly, but his eyes were working, sharp and deliberate, tracking reactions instead of appearances, noting who hesitated, who adapted, who didn’t seem to realize they were being watched.

And finally, Retsuko, who seemed to arrive half a second behind everyone else, her smaller frame stiffening as awareness caught up with her. Her paws hovered near her sides, uncertain where to settle, ears twitching once as she looked from one figure to another before dropping her gaze briefly, already trying to understand where she fit before anyone else decided it for her.

The silence stretched.

Not empty – observed.

“Well!”

The voice cut across the stage cleanly, bright, delighted, landing just after the moment had fully settled.

At the center, where there had been nothing, now stood a man in a pinstripe suit, hands clasped neatly behind his back, smile already in place as though he had been waiting for them to arrive on cue.

“Look at that,” James “Jimothy” Nyarlathotep said, glancing between them with open satisfaction. “Twelve contestants, fully briefed, minimally panicked – always nice when that lines up.”

“Well then,” Jimothy said, the words settling lightly over the stage as he took a slow step forward, polished shoes making no sound against the reflective surface. His hands remained clasped neatly behind his back, posture effortless, like this arrangement – the stage, the contestants, the attention – had always been his by default. 

“Since everyone is now operating with the same baseline understanding, we can move directly into our first phase.” His gaze moved across them, not lingering, not skipping, simply… noting. Not who they were, but how they stood, how they held themselves now that they knew. “You’ve already been provided with the essentials,” he continued, tone conversational, almost pleasant. “You are here to be observed. To be evaluated. To be ranked.” A slight tilt of his head followed, just enough to suggest the next part mattered more. “And, eventually… to be removed.” The word didn’t linger. He didn’t let it. 

“But before we get to anything so final,” he added lightly, one gloved hand lifting just slightly as though adjusting the pacing of the moment itself, “we need to establish something far more important.” Another step, closer now – not toward any one of them, but toward the center of them all. “How you wish to be seen.”

You see, reputation is not a static quality. It is not what you *are*.” His gaze flicked briefly, almost idly, across a few of them – long enough to register, not long enough to single out. “It is what others decide you are, based on what you choose to show them.” His hand lowered again, returning neatly behind his back. “And so, your first task is remarkably simple.”

The stage responded before he finished speaking. Not dramatically, not loudly, just… precisely. The polished floor beneath them separated, not breaking, not cracking, but dividing into four distinct sections, each marked not by walls, but by subtle shifts in light and tone. The air itself seemed to differentiate them, each space carrying a slightly different weight, a slightly different expectation, a presence that didn’t impose itself so much as wait to be interpreted. Jimothy didn’t look down at it. He didn’t need to.

“You will choose,” he said, “where you belong.” A small pause followed, not empty, but deliberate. “Each section represents a trait. A lens through which you may be judged… and through which you may judge others.” His gaze moved again, slower this time. “Trust.” One section warmed slightly, the light softening just enough to feel inviting without quite being comfortable. “Power.” Another sharpened, the edges of its light more defined, more exact. “Adaptability.” A third shifted subtly, the boundaries harder to pin down, the space feeling just slightly less fixed than the others. “And Independence.” The final section didn’t brighten, didn’t sharpen – it simply stood apart, its presence defined not by what it offered, but by what it refused to rely on, its light neither welcoming nor distant, existing on its own terms in a way that didn’t ask to be chosen.

Jimothy’s smile widened, just slightly.

“You may interpret these however you like,” he said. “That is, in fact, the point.” His eyes flicked once more across the group, catching reactions, hesitation, calculation. “Once chosen, your position will determine your initial grouping. Your first table, if you prefer something more… traditional.” He let that settle. “From there, you will participate in your first challenge. A game that will require you to demonstrate exactly what you believe you represent.” No elaboration. Not yet. “And while you play,” he added, almost as an afterthought, “the rest will watch.” 

That was where the weight shifted. “Observe carefully,” he continued, voice still light, still pleasant. “You will not be scoring arbitrarily. Your perceptions will have consequences.” A small tilt of his head followed, expression brightening again as though the tension hadn’t just been introduced. “Oh – and one final clarification. While you are here, you cannot harm one another. Not physically. Not directly.” A pause. “Everything else,” he added, just a touch softer, “remains available to you.”

Then, just as easily, the warmth returned.

“So,” Jimothy said, stepping back, giving them the stage as though it had ever belonged to anyone else, “take a moment. Decide how you wish to be seen.” His smile sharpened, ever so slightly, teeth just a bit too perfect in the light. “First impressions,” he said, “tend to last.”
